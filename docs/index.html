Motivation

Through my professional journey as a consultant in multilateral and governmental institutions, I have been exposed to the power of phrases and slogans, and observed how they suddenly appear in the policy discourse, and – at least for some time – take the center stage.
Furthermore, in the institutions I have worked for (World Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, G20, the EU, and the Italian Government), catch-phrases and slogans end up mobilizing sizable funding decisions, thus determining which policies or programs will get prioritized.

Examples of slogans ins the development economics arena

Just to stick to a sector I am familiar with, in the field of development economics, we have seen the rise of slogans such as:

FUNDING AID

  • Boost shared prosperity
  • Maximizing Finance for Development (MFD)
  • Program for Results (PforR)
  • Results-Based Financing (RBF) / Results-Based Aid (RBA)

INCLUSION

  • South-South Cooperation
  • Gender Mainstreaming
  • Leave No One Behind (EU)

SUSTAINABILITY, ENVIRONMENT

  • Sustainable Development / Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
  • Environmental, social, and governance (ESG)
  • Mainstreaming climate action
  • 3 zeros (0 poverty, 0 net carbon emissions, 0 net loss of natural capital)
  • Green, Resilient, and Inclusive Development (GRID)
  • Build Back Better

The curious case of lexical reduction/modification/domestication

Another element that makes this investigation even more intriguing (as it occurs also in the field of public policy) is that, often, the meaning of words/phrases progressively detaches from reality and/or from their originally intended meaning, where some “revised meaning” (more or less explicit) starts to live journey of its own.
For example, I was really struck the first time I realized how the Latin motto I had heard since childhood: “Mens sana in corpore sano”, was actually a partial quotation used in 1861 by the Englishman John Hulley, as a motto for his Liverpool Athletic Club. In fact, it was extrapolated from a longer sentence by Juvenal poet: “Orandum est ut sit mens sana in corpore sano” (Satire X, 356). Taken in its entirety, the statement has quite a different meaning than its widely used shortened version: for one thing, it revolved around prayer and not fitness!

This phenomenon is clearly relevant and consequence-ridden if, as Giovanni Gentile pointed out in “Sommario di pedagogia come scienza filosofica”, “Language is not a garment of thought: it is thought’s own body” (Garbini 2003, p3), i.e. language does not merely communicate our ideas to others, but it is also the tool of our thought, it provides wings for it to fly. D’Auria put it in another way (D’Auria 2024), asserting that language is “performative”, in that it colors, deforms, produces reality. For this reason, it is necessarily a divisive instrument because, as soon as it is able to categorize, it is also capable of separating (good from bad, truth from lie, etc.). Knowledge itself, as much as it may scare the proponents of political correctness, lives on categories and categories, including linguistic ones, must precisely distinguish and correctly identify the ontological characteristics of what we are talking about.

So what?

In sum, for a while now I have wondered why and by which mechanisms such phrases/slogans/acronyms become so prevalent in a given forum at some specific point in time. The problem is that if you are (as I always was) just a small cog in very complex institutional machine, it is difficult to capture the (often intangible) dynamics of this process. Therefore, I decided to investigate this phenomenon with a “reverse engineering” approach, starting from the corpora of documents themselves to see if I could identify some patterns or trends over time.

Why focus on the World Bank?

The World Bank simply represents a convenient “learning case” for me because:

  1. Its documents (related to lending projects and other publications) are widely available and openly accessible.
  2. The Bank has a long history and a vast amount of public digitized documents, which allows for a longitudinal analysis.
  3. The Bank’s sectorial and thematic coverage is very broad, which allows for an interesting analysis.
  4. I am familiar with the Bank’s documents’ nature, structure and jargon, having worked there for several years.
